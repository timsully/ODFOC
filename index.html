Oregon Detention Facility Oversight Commission

Lighting the shadows: independent oversight for human rights

A citizen-led Oregon measure to create an expert commission with transparency, accountability, and jurisdiction over state, federal, and private detention facilities—including ICE and Border Patrol sites on Oregon soil.

What your documents say—in plain language

The problem

Federal agencies (ICE, CBP) and contractors operate detention and temporary facilities in Oregon with no state oversight. Your materials describe reports of medical neglect, abuse, and harm to people in care—including children in “Tender Care” settings—while evidence can be hard to surface without independent monitors.

The solution

Initiative 2026-081 would establish the Oregon Detention Facility Oversight Commission: a three-member, expert body to inspect conditions, publish findings, and route evidence of abuse to appropriate law enforcement or courts.

Who serves

Exactly three roles: a founding chair tied to the chief petitioner’s grassroots leadership; a professional journalist for public, clear reporting; and a licensed medical professional to oversee health and “Tender Care” standards. Vacancies for journalist and medical seats are filled by Governor + Attorney General appointment, or by self-petition with 100 registered-voter signatures.

Scope & transparency

Jurisdiction covers state, federal, and private facilities—processing centers, hold rooms, “Tender Care” sites, and soft-sided structures. Inspections are unannounced, with a mandated live public video feed using real-time blurring to protect privacy and HIPAA-related concerns.

Evidence & funding

When abuse or neglect is found, the commission secures evidence and transfers it to local police, county sheriff, Oregon State Police, or a circuit court judge. Funding comes from oversight fees on facility operators, pursuit of federal reimbursement (including references to 2025 federal legislation in your draft), with a general-fund advance that your text says should be repaid when federal dollars arrive.

Ballot path

Your fact sheet states the campaign needs 1,000 sponsorship signatures from registered Oregon voters to move the measure toward review and a possible November public vote. Chief petitioner listed in your sheets: Shawn Mauldin, Salem, OR (A Helping Hand Locksmith).

Official sponsorship signatures use the state e-sheet. To count toward Initiative 2026-081, Oregon voters should use the Secretary of State’s electronic signature sheet (with the full initiative text attached, as the sheet requires).
